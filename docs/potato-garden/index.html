Beds

  • Squash Bed
    • Zucchini
    • Sunflower or Corn (optional, planted with the squash - one grows low, the other grows high)
    • Cucumber (may want to use these as shade for lettuce, but I think this was the plan last we talked)
    • Dill (right next to cucumbers)
  • Lettuce Bed
    • Lettuce - succession - you'll want different varieties of lettuce for heat tolerance, even within a broader type. But maybe best to just see how it goes this year. You may want to try some napa cabbage, it's a brassica which are supposed to conflict with lettuce, but seeing conflicting information about the conflicts (not sure if it's all brassica or the nature of the conflict) and napa is more heat tolerant.
    • Carrots - succession
    • Fast growing vine as shade on trellis e.g. pole beans, mango-melon, cucumbers
  • Tomato and Pepper Bed
    • Tomato - Slicing - Key here is avoiding "determinate" varieties - they produce all their tomatoes in one shot. Great for sauce, not great for full season production. If you want a smaller bush, you're probably looking for "semi-determinate" - basically a dwarf vining tomato. But most heirlooms are simply "indeterminate" vining and can get pretty large.
    • Pepper
  • Herbs - TBD - note that many of these will need to be seed started, but heavily variety-dependent, some can be perennials in your zone so may be worth potting
  • Companion plants - Herbs work for this (e.g. Basil, Oregano in tomato/pepper bed, Dill with cucumbers), also recommend nasturtiums and marigolds. Both flowers are fine to direct seed, but both will flower all summer so earlier start isn't a bad idea. Nasturtium is more attractive to pests than other plants, Marigold repels most bugs, so two approaches to the same problem.
  • Fall plants - not covering here, but garlic and onions are generally planted in the fall and come up in the spring

Equipment

Just briefly, just things you might benefit from (I have a good bit of this already, probably have enough to share depending on the item)
  • Digging fork/similar - a shovel may work, only need it start of season so happy to lend
  • Heat mat for seed germination - some seeds won't germinate at lower temperatures, probably don't need after the plants have their first leaves
  • Lights for seed starting - some seeds need light to germinate e.g. oregano, but primarily this is for growth after they've germinated. Should be *close* and *bright*.
  • Timer to control lights for seed starting - plants grow at night, need to cycle

  • Plant ties
  • Tomato support - you can just have mine, I'm not growing tomatoes
  • Pepper support (maybe, I've never had peppers that successful and peppers aren't heavy like slicing tomatoes, check w/ Romes)

Seeds

Big box stores are notorious for selling you things that won't grow where they're selling them. The internet isn't better, so y'know, read. Or outsource it to AI. Biggest thing I can say is pay attention to the seed counts/weight of the packets. Plants with larger seeds end up with fewer seeds in a packet. Similar with rare varieties. On your scale probably won't be a huge issue, just every plant is 2-3 seeds (you thin [kill] the losers), if the packet only has 9 seeds and costs 5 bucks....
  • Snake River Seeds - *all* the seeds they sell are from plants grown in Idaho/nearby. Not all landrace, but should be hardy. (landrace is letting the plants fight the weeds, bugs and each other on their own - idea is the ones that survive with the traits you're looking for should be very tough in that zone)
  • Botanical Interests - prices aren't great but otherwise been happy with these guys
  • Burpee - the classic, I don't think I've ordered from them but their website is pretty useful
  • Tomorrow Seeds - first year I'm using them so can't recommend yet, but prices are good and website is great

Schedule

Using the early dates as the reference. Note that all dates are just a guideline, +/- a week is assumed. Also not covering watering, that'll be up to your system and weather - for seed starting and growing.

Frost

Note that last frost dates are their own thing. Two different sites will give you two different dates, even though they're using the same data. E.g. Farmers Almanac has Apr 30, where Garden.org has May 26, into June. Simple fact is you can get frost in your yard and a mile away they won't. You'll have to adjust for your yard. You may want to pick up some cloches or row covers just to be safe. The cheap cloches are cheap, and both can be reused. I doubt it'll be an issue this year. Worst case you just wait, but every week the plants aren't in the garden can be a week you lose of harvest. You have a long enough season you can play it safe this year and adjust for next. Just be ready to eat some green tomatoes.

Soil Temperature

Beyond frost, the actual temperature of the soil affects growth. For seeds directly sowed, it can prevent germination as well. Some plants grow better in cold soil e.g. lettuce, and start to bolt (focus on reproduction over growth) in the heat. Other plants won't grow in the cold e.g. tomato, or grow more slowly in the cold e.g. squash. So it's something to pay attention to. You have raised beds, which heat up more quickly because of the increased surface area. You can manage a similar trick on a smaller scale by building small mounds for your seeds that need that warmth. You may want to avoid that for plants with shallower roots like corn, though.
